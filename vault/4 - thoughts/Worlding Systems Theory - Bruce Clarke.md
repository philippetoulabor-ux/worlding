This text explores **neocybernetic systems theory** and its application to the concept of a **globalized world society**, primarily through the lens of sociologist Niklas Luhmann. It argues that modern society is a singular, **encompassing system of communication** that functions through specialized subsystems—like law, economy, and science—which operate according to their own internal logic while remaining closed off from their environment. The author contrasts this "one-world" systemic view with the **pluriverse and fractiverse**, concepts from scholars like Arturo Escobar and John Law who suggest that reality is actually composed of **multiple, co-existing worlds** enacted through diverse human practices. Finally, the text suggests that while social systems are often blind to their surroundings, they can be "nudged" by human consciousness and the **ecological urgency of Gaia**, potentially forcing the global system to adapt to the material realities of our **planetary environment**.
