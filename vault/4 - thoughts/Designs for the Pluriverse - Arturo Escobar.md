Arturo Escobar’s **Designs for the Pluriverse** advocates for a fundamental **ontological reorientation** of design, moving it away from the destructive, rationalistic traditions of **patriarchal capitalist modernity**. The text argues that design is never neutral; rather, it is a **world-making practice** that historically has enforced a single, globalized way of life while suppressing cultural and biological diversity. Escobar proposes a shift toward **relationality**, an ethos of **radical interdependence** where the "double movement" of design acknowledges that as we create our tools and environments, they simultaneously **design us back**.

To counter the "defuturing" effects of modern industry, the author champions **design for transitions**, a framework aimed at fostering a **pluriverse**—a "world where many worlds fit." This approach draws inspiration from the **autonomous design** practices of indigenous and Afro-descendant communities in Latin America, who use design to defend their territories and "Life Projects." By centering the **communal** and the "politics of the relational," Escobar seeks to empower local groups to self-organize and reclaim their **cultural and ecological autonomy**.

Ultimately, the book serves as a bridge between **critical social theory** and professional practice, urging designers to become activists for a **convivial** and sustainable future. Escobar envisions a transition from a civilization based on domination to one rooted in a **"biology of love"** and mutual care. This transformation requires designers to move beyond mere object creation and instead facilitate the **collective making of worlds** that honor the Earth and the multiplicity of human experience.
