WORLDING

Worlding - as a practice in design

There is no single, neutral, pre-given reality. Instead, there are many equally valid Worlds and they are made, not discovered. Science, art, everyday perception, music… all of them construct their Worlds through their own symbols, categories, and systems of description.

As a child, I was obsessed with basic LEGO bricks. I never wanted a pre-designed Fire Station or a Star Wars Death Star; I always preferred the simple blocks. The possibility of building anything I could imagine out of this neutral matter had a huge pull on me. My excitement didn’t come from just creating a isolated new shape out of these blocks. It came from inventing rules that transformed the space around it. I would look at the carpet in my bedroom and decide: "This is water, and the figures can't swim." With that one simple rule, a World was born. It provided a rich context that gave every possible new shape a set of requirements it needed to fulfill in order to hold value and have a reason to exist. My mind kept coming up with new artefacts.. bridges, boats, or houses on stilts,… all of them followed and further defined the rules and logic of this newly created reality. With every new artefact the World got easier to grasp for a person that’s not the creator. At some point playmates/collaborators can enter the picture. With the rules set, everyone could come in and develop characters, story lines and objects that inhabit that reality.

This is the essence of Worlding: creating a generative ruleset that, once established, takes on a life of its own. In some cases it can even become fully independent of its creator, functioning as an open engine that continuously can produce new artefacts, behaviors, and narratives.

In design, we can use this urge to shift our focus away from shaping isolated, individual objects. Instead, we can design rule-based, open systems. By establishing the foundational laws of a World, we allow it to take on a life of its own and evolve autonomously, through user behavior, networking or simulation. The designer no longer just shapes the final product; they design the ecosystem that allows new products to emerge.

In my bachelor project i want to develop methods that enable the process of Worlding in the context of design. My goal is to design a new World by constructing its foundational framework, setting its rules and finally producing some artefacts as examples.

creating a new World (first attempt)

Framework/Rules:

two sheets of material stuck through each other, supporting each other to hold weight.

everything else can be seen as a variable. So material, shape (i.e. of the outline), size, weight,…

this gives enormous freedom.. objects in this world could range from a 3 person sofa (with foam pillows) to a small phone stand for the desk

Goal (does a world even need a defined goal? If yes.. why?):

Consumer → Collaborator , change the dynamic from people just being consumers/customers of your product towards people being your collaborators on something new
